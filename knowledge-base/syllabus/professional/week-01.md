### Key concepts

**1. This Is Not Another Software Tool**

Previous waves of enterprise software — ERPs, CRMs, mobile apps — automated tasks within fixed decision trees. The system did exactly what it was told to do, no more. Agents are different: they can reason about novel situations, make judgements, combine tools, and adapt their approach based on results.

This changes the cost curve for knowledge work. A human analyst doing market research: 8 hours, one person, one research task. An agent doing market research: 5 minutes, $0.05 in API costs, runs 24/7. The analyst's job changes: they specify what research to do, evaluate the output, and act on it.

Professionals who understand how to specify, evaluate, and manage agents will be irreplaceable. Professionals who do not will be replaced by those who do.

**2. The Digital FTE**

A Digital FTE is an AI agent configured to perform a specific role — a consistent bundle of tasks that a human employee would handle. It is not a chatbot. It takes actions: it reads emails, writes reports, queries databases, sends notifications, and escalates when needed.

Cost comparison for a Kampala business (illustrative):
- Human data entry clerk: 800,000 UGX/month + benefits
- Digital FTE (data entry agent): ~50,000 UGX/month (API costs) with no sick days, no training overhead, runs at night

The Digital FTE does not replace people it respects — it replaces the drudge work that prevents talented people from doing high-value work.

**3. Why Uganda Now**

Uganda's formal economy is growing. NSSF has 4M+ contributors. Kampala has 200,000+ registered businesses. The National Identification and Registration Authority (NIRA) issues millions of IDs. These institutions run complex administrative workflows with high staff costs. The cost of AI agents is falling 10x every 18 months. The developers who build these agents for East African institutions will build the infrastructure of the next decade.

**4. The Professional Track's Role**

Developers build agents. Professionals commission them, manage them, and hold them accountable. You do not need to write code. You need to:
- Write clear specifications that developers can build
- Evaluate whether what was built does what you specified
- Identify when an agent is wrong and articulate why
- Make the business case for investment

This is not a technical role. It is a governance and strategy role. It is the role the next generation of Kampala managers must own.

### Kampala example

**MTN Uganda Customer Service Transformation**

MTN Uganda handles thousands of customer service calls daily. Common issues: SIM swap requests, data bundle queries, MoMo transaction disputes, network complaints.

An agent configuration for this workflow:
- Agent receives transcribed customer query (via IVR or text)
- Looks up account, recent transactions, and common resolution patterns
- Resolves 70% of cases without human involvement
- Escalates 30% to the appropriate human agent with context pre-populated

For a Professional manager at MTN Uganda, the skill is not building this agent — it is writing the spec that tells the developer team what the agent must do, reviewing the output quality, and deciding when escalation is required. That is a management function, and it requires deep knowledge of how MTN's customer service actually works.

### Common questions

**Q: I'm not a developer. Can I really manage AI agents?**
A: Yes — and your non-technical background is an advantage. Developers know how to build agents. You know what agents should do to create value in a business. The combination is what ships useful products. The most dangerous person in AI is a developer with no business context building solutions to the wrong problems.

**Q: Will AI replace my job?**
A: The accurate answer: AI will replace some tasks in your job. The tasks most at risk are routine, well-defined, and information-processing. The tasks least at risk are relationship-dependent, judgment-heavy, and politically complex. Your job is to move toward the second category.

**Q: How fast is this changing?**
A: Very fast. Gemini and Claude capabilities improve roughly every 3 months with meaningful jumps. A task that was too expensive or unreliable in January may be cost-effective and reliable in July. This means you must revisit your agent strategy regularly — not once.

### Practice exercise

**Exercise 1.1 — Workflow Audit**

List 10 tasks you or your team perform regularly that are primarily information-processing (reading, writing, calculating, looking things up, copying data between systems, sending standard notifications).

For each task, rate it on two scales:
- **Volume**: How many times per week does this happen? (1-5, 6-20, 20+)
- **Variability**: How different is each instance from the last? (Low, Medium, High)

High volume + low variability = strong agent candidate.

Pick your top 3 candidates and write one sentence describing who currently does the task and what they would do instead if the agent handled it.
